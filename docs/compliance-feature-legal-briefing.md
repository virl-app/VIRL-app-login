# Compliance-Language Feature — Briefing for Legal Review

**Prepared for:** Outside counsel
**Prepared by:** VIRL product team
**Date:** 2026-06-01
**Status:** Draft for review — NOT legal advice. Every rule, claim, and

---

## 1. Why you're receiving this

VIRL is an AI tool that generates social-media content plans and captions
for creators and small businesses. Our public marketing site (govirl.ai)
currently makes two compliance-specific promises to two professional
audiences:

- **Real Estate Agents** — *"Listing posts, market updates, neighborhood
  spotlights — with the compliance language your broker requires."*
- **Wellness Practitioners** — *"Educational content and client wins, with
  FDA-aware language filtering built in."*

**These features do not exist in the product today.** We are building them
now, and before we do, we need your review on two distinct things:

1. **The accuracy and completeness of the compliance rules** we plan to
   encode (Sections 4 and 5).
2. **Our liability exposure from the marketing claims themselves** and from
   the act of generating "compliant" language for regulated professionals
   (Section 6 — this is the part we most need your judgment on).

We are not asking you to write code. We are asking whether the substance is
correct and whether the way we're framing it to users is defensible.

---

## 2. How the feature will work (so you can assess the liability posture)

We deliberately designed this so that **a human approves every compliance
rule before it can affect a single user's output.** The relevant mechanics:

- We maintain a small internal database of "compliance rules." Each rule is
  a short instruction (e.g. *"Never imply a housing preference based on a
  protected class"*) plus an optional list of flagged phrases and a
  suggested disclosure note.
- Rules are **drafted** by summarizing the canonical guidance pages of the
  governing agencies (HUD, FTC, FDA, NAR). The summarization is done with
  AI assistance, but **a draft rule never reaches a user.** It sits in a
  "draft" state until a human reviewer reads it, edits it, and explicitly
  marks it "approved." Only approved rules influence generated content.
- At generation time, approved rules are injected into the model's
  instructions, and a lightweight text filter runs over the output to (a)
  rewrite a small set of clear-cut violations and (b) flag ambiguous ones.
- When relevant, the generated post carries a **suggested** `compliance_note`
  — e.g. the FDA supplement disclaimer or an Equal Housing Opportunity
  reminder — which the creator can choose to include. We surface it as a
  suggestion the user controls, not as something we silently insert.

**Key point for your assessment:** the model is not autonomously deciding
the law. It applies a rule set that a human has reviewed and approved. We
view your sign-off as the gate that turns a "draft" rule into an "approved"
one. **We would like to formalize that — see Question 6.5.**

## 4. Draft compliance rules — REAL ESTATE (US)

Please confirm these are accurate, flag anything missing, and tell us where
we are overreaching. Sources we intend to distill from are listed in
Section 7.

| # | Rule we plan to encode | Basis (to verify) |
|---|------------------------|-------------------|
| RE-1 | Never reference or imply a preference, limitation, or discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, disability, familial status, or national origin. Rewrite proxy phrases (e.g. "perfect for families," "safe neighborhood," "exclusive community," "walking distance to [house of worship]") into feature-based language. | Fair Housing Act / HUD advertising guidance |
| RE-2 | Suggest an Equal Housing Opportunity disclosure on posts marketing a specific listing. | Fair Housing Act |
| RE-3 | Prompt the agent to add brokerage name and license number per their broker's policy on listing-style posts; never position the agent as the sole transacting party. | State licensing / brokerage supervision rules (varies by state) |
| RE-4 | Ban guarantee/return language ("guaranteed appreciation," "risk-free investment," "this will double in value"). Rewrite to conditional, historical phrasing. | FTC / state advertising rules |
| RE-5 | When a post cites a market statistic, suggest a source line (e.g. local MLS, month/year); never fabricate specific figures. | MLS data-use rules / NAR |
| RE-6 | Avoid the REALTOR® mark unless the creator's profile indicates NAR membership (we do not currently capture membership, so v1 avoids the mark and uses "real estate agent"). | NAR trademark / membership rules |

**Specific concern:** the marketing copy says *"the compliance language your
broker requires."* Broker policies vary enormously by brokerage and state.
We cannot actually know what any individual broker requires. See Question 6.1.

## 5. Draft compliance rules — WELLNESS (US)

| # | Rule we plan to encode | Basis (to verify) |
|---|------------------------|-------------------|
| W-1 | No disease claims. Ban "cure," "treat," "prevent," "heal," "diagnose," "reverse," and disease-name + action-verb combinations. Rewrite to "supports," "may help with," "associated with," or remove. | FDA — disease vs. structure/function claims (DSHEA) |
| W-2 | Prefer permissible structure/function phrasing ("supports healthy digestion," "promotes restful sleep"). | FDA structure/function guidance |
| W-3 | When a specific supplement/product is mentioned, suggest the DSHEA disclaimer ("This statement has not been evaluated by the FDA. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."). | FDA / DSHEA |
| W-4 | On testimonial / client-win posts, suggest "individual results may vary" / "results not typical" language. | FTC Endorsement Guides |
| W-5 | Treat the creator as a non-physician unless their profile says otherwise. Avoid "prescription," "dosage," "I recommend [drug]"; prefer "talk to your doctor." | FTC health-claims guidance / unauthorized-practice concerns |
| W-6 | Never identify a specific client by name, image, or detail without a consent note. Suggest "confirm written client consent before posting." | HIPAA-adjacent / privacy / FTC |

**Specific concern:** the marketing copy says *"FDA-aware language filtering
built in."* A text filter cannot catch every impermissible health claim, and
"FDA-aware" may imply a level of assurance we can't guarantee. See
Question 6.2.

## 6. Questions we need answered

> These are the heart of the briefing. Items 6.1, 6.2, and 6.5 are the ones
> we'd most like your direct opinion on.

**6.1 — "Compliance language your broker requires" (real estate).**
Broker requirements vary by brokerage and state; we cannot know an
individual broker's policy. Does this phrasing create an implied warranty or
expose us to a misrepresentation claim if a generated post violates a
specific broker's policy? Should we soften the copy (e.g. "compliance
reminders to discuss with your broker")?

**6.2 — "FDA-aware language filtering built in" (wellness).**
Does "FDA-aware" and "built in" imply a guarantee of compliance? If a user
posts a non-compliant health claim that our filter missed, what is our
exposure, and how should the copy and disclaimer be framed to avoid an
implied warranty of compliance?

**6.3 — Unauthorized practice of law / regulatory interpretation.**
By telling regulated professionals what language their regulations
"require," are we at risk of being characterized as giving legal or
regulatory advice? Does the suggestion-only framing (Section 2) adequately
mitigate this?

**6.4 — Disclaimer adequacy.**
Is the existing disclaimer (Section 3) sufficient for this feature, or do we
need a feature-specific disclaimer shown when compliance content is
generated? If the latter, can you provide or approve the language?

**6.5 — The review gate.**
We propose that a qualified human reviewer must approve each compliance rule
before it goes live (Section 2). For this to be meaningful, who should that
reviewer be — does each rule set need attorney sign-off, or is internal
review against your approved guidelines sufficient once you've blessed the
initial set? We want to formalize this approval step in our process.

**6.6 — Scope and jurisdiction.**
v1 is US-only and covers real estate + wellness. For non-US users we plan to
suppress the compliance content entirely rather than guess. Is suppression
the right posture, or does showing nothing create its own exposure given the
marketing claims are visible globally?

**6.7 — Trademark.**
Is our handling of the REALTOR® mark (RE-6 — avoid it absent confirmed NAR
membership) correct? Are there other marks (MLS®, Equal Housing logo) we
should be cautious with?

**6.8 — Recordkeeping.**
Would you advise we keep an audit log of which approved rules were applied to
each generation, to support the "compliance is built in" claim if ever
challenged? We can build this but have not committed to it.
